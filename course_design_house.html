Text description of this diagram
Learning Activities — the roof
The roof sits on top of the walls and holds it all together — but it is designed last, after the foundation and walls are in place. Learning activities (readings, lectures, labs, discussions, projects) are selected and sequenced to enable learners to reach the objectives and perform on the assessments. The roof's job is to shelter everything underneath, not to drive the design.
UbD Stage 3: plan learning experiences and instruction. Wiggins & McTighe argue that teachers too often start here — the classic "twin sins" of activity-focused or coverage-focused design.
Formative Assessment — one wall
Formative assessments are the ongoing checks built into the learning process — low-stakes quizzes, drafts, discussions, exit tickets, peer review. Like a structural wall, they provide lateral support and keep the course aligned as it unfolds. They exist to inform instruction and give learners feedback while there's still time to adjust.
UbD Stage 2 asks: what evidence will show that learning is happening in progress? Formative evidence helps both instructor and learner see where they are relative to the goals.
Summative Assessment — the other wall
Summative assessments are the culminating demonstrations of learning — final projects, exams, portfolios, presentations. As the other load-bearing wall, they provide symmetrical structural support. In backward design, you define these before you plan instruction, because the endpoint shapes everything else.
Wiggins & McTighe emphasize authentic performance tasks as the gold standard for summative evidence — tasks that ask learners to apply knowledge in meaningful, real-world contexts.
Learning Objectives — the floor
The floor rests directly on the foundation and gives the house a surface to stand on. Learning objectives translate the transformational goal into specific, measurable outcomes — what learners will be able to know, do, or value by the end of the course. They provide the platform everything else stands on.
UbD Stage 1: identifying desired results. Wiggins & McTighe recommend framing objectives around transfer, meaning-making, and acquisition.
Transformational Goal — the foundation
The foundation supports the entire structure. A transformational goal articulates the deepest purpose of a course: not just what learners will know, but who they will become. It answers the question, "Why does this course exist?" Everything else is built on top of it. Without a solid foundation, the house collapses.
Wiggins & McTighe call this the "enduring understanding" — the big ideas and core processes that transfer to new contexts long after the unit ends.

Based on Wiggins & McTighe, Understanding by Design (2005) · CIPD / Adaptive Edge Institute
